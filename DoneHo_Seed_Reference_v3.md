# DoneHo — Curated Pattern Reference Document (v3)

**Purpose:** source material for NudgeAgent (Opportunity Map, Smart Spend,
Day Boosters). This version fixes a real gap in v2: it stopped at
classifying tasks by attention tier and never did the actual work of
inventing genuine combination patterns. This version does that — for
people in situations beyond the founder's own (working mother) context,
since DoneHo needs to serve gig workers, students, retirees, single
professionals, caregivers of elderly parents, night-shift workers, and
more, not just one life situation.

**Source tiers:**

- 🟢 **FOUNDER-VERIFIED** — the founder's own real experience.
- 🔵 **AI-DISCOVERED** — genuinely new patterns invented here by applying
the founder's own reasoning *style* (find real, non-obvious overlap;
respect safety; respect protected time) to different people's
situations. Reasonable, creative starting points — not personally
verified, should be corrected as real usage data comes in.

Terminology: **Combinability Tier** (Exclusive/Partial/Ambient) is a
task-intrinsic default, separate from DoneHo's existing personalized
`FocusLevel`. The user's real FocusLevel always wins if the two conflict.

---

## PART A — General Rules

*(unchanged from v2 — see prior version; kept here for completeness)*

1. Never pair two Exclusive tasks, never pair two Partial tasks.
2. The test is felt experience, not technical compatibility.
3. Physical safety overrides efficiency — voice-only during self-driving;
full engagement fine as a passenger or on public transport.
4. Some tasks are Exclusive on principle (meditation, prayer, digital
detox, protected family time) — never suggest combining these, even
if a plausible pairing exists.
5. Caregiving pairs only through genuine overlap, never as a workaround.
6. Every Smart Spend suggestion needs real trade-off reasoning.
7. Favor reusable systems over repeated daily decisions.
8. Generalize by pattern-shape, not literal task name.

## PART B — Genuinely New Combination Patterns, Across Different Lives

This is the actual inventive work — organized by the *kind* of person
facing the task, since the same task (e.g., "commute," "exercise,"
"journaling") plays out completely differently depending on someone's
real situation. Each pattern names *why* it's genuinely non-obvious, not
just that a pairing exists.

### For gig / delivery workers

1. 🔵 **Unpredictable dead time between rides, not a fixed commute.**
Unlike a scheduled commute, a delivery worker's downtime is
unpredictable in both timing and length — so the right suggestion
isn't "listen to a podcast on your commute," it's short, self-contained
audio chunks (5-min language drills, single-concept explainers)
triggered by app-detected idle time, not a calendar slot. The
*mechanism* has to differ, not just the content.
2. 🔵 **Don't double-count fitness.** If someone's job already involves
substantial biking/walking, DoneHo should recognize the existing task
as already satisfying a stated fitness goal, rather than layering a
separate "workout" milestone on top — avoiding a real overload risk
that a generic system would miss. *This is a new category of pattern
entirely: recognizing when a goal is already met by something the user
is doing anyway, not finding a new combination.*
3. 🔵 **Irregular income → event-triggered financial check-ins.** A fixed
weekly budget-review slot is easy to skip when income itself is
irregular. Instead, trigger a short financial check-in immediately
after each payment/invoice is received — a naturally-occurring event
makes the habit stick better than an arbitrary calendar slot.

### For caregivers of elderly parents

1. 🔵 **Accompanying a parent's prescribed walk doubles as the caregiver's
own cardio.** Same underlying principle as the founder's mirror-play
insight (genuine overlap, not a workaround) — but applied to an
entirely different caregiving relationship (adult child, not new
parent).
2. 🔵 **Turn a parent's rehab/mobility exercises into a shared,
game-like moment with a grandchild** ("let's both touch our toes") —
improves the parent's exercise compliance through company, while
giving the grandchild activity real structure. Three real goals
(elder health, intergenerational bonding, child engagement) genuinely
overlap in one moment.
3. 🔵 **One shared reminder system for both the caregiver's own health
habits and the parent's medication schedule**, instead of two separate
ones — reduces the caregiver's real mental overhead of tracking two
people's routines. A systemization insight (Rule 7), applied
specifically to dual-person households, not just one person's day.

### For students (especially hostel/PG life)

1. 🔵 **Laundromat/shared-laundry wait time is genuinely unpredictable in
length** — unlike a fixed commute, it can end abruptly. Suggest short,
interruptible flashcard-style revision (not long-form audio) so
nothing gets lost if the wait ends early.
2. 🔵 **Study material overload → audio conversion at scale**, per the
founder's own 50-PDF pattern — but note this generalizes strongly to
*any* student drowning in reading material, not just exam-specific
prep — the pattern is "convert volume to passive-consumable audio,"
not tied to any one subject.

### For remote/WFH employees

1. 🔵 **Convert non-screen-sharing 1:1 calls into walking meetings.** For
remote workers specifically, this directly counters the sedentary
risk WFH creates — genuinely different context from a chore-pairing
suggestion, since it's replacing a *work* task's format, not adding a
personal task alongside a chore.

### For solo / single-living professionals

1. 🔵 **Batch cooking (e.g., Sunday meal-prep) while video-calling a
friend.** Cooking for one often feels inefficient enough that people
skip it — pairing it with a standing social call turns an
often-skipped, isolating chore into a genuinely anticipated one. The
the combination axis here is *social*, not audio-learning — a different
kind of pairing than most of this document covers.
2. 🔵 **Introverts building a relationship/social goal:** pair the
socializing with a shared *activity* (a walk, a class, cooking
together) rather than unstructured conversation — connection happens
"sideways," which is often genuinely easier for people who find
direct small talk draining. This is an insight about *how* to
combine, not just *that* two things can combine.

### For public-transport commuters (distinct from self-driving)

1. 🔵 **The self-driving safety guardrail (voice-only) does NOT apply on
trains, buses, or metros.** Worth stating explicitly as its own case,
not just an exception — commuters using public transport can engage
with visual content (reading, video lectures) that a self-driver
genuinely cannot. Two very different suggestions for what looks like
"the same" commute task.

### For new parents (a sharper version of timing logic)

1. 🔵 **Use a baby's nap window as the unlock condition for errands**,
not a simultaneous-activity pairing. Many parents already structure
grocery runs around a stroller nap specifically because the baby is
asleep — the actual mechanism is *timing-triggered*, not
*concurrent-activity* — a genuinely different pattern-type than most
of this document, worth DoneHo recognizing as its own case.

### For people managing a chronic health condition

1. 🔵 **Habit-stack a new low-adherence task onto an existing
100%-reliable one** — e.g., a blood sugar check immediately before or
after brushing teeth. This is a distinct mechanism from simultaneous
combination — an *anchor habit*, borrowed from established behavior-
change practice, applied here for the first time in this document.

### For small business owners / freelancers

1. 🔵 **Capture expenses at the exact moment of the transaction**
(photograph the receipt immediately at point of purchase) rather than
batching for later — prevents the classic "shoebox of receipts"
failure mode. Immediate-capture beats batch-capture specifically
because the batching step is where this task usually dies.

### For people with mobility limitations

1. 🔵 **Seated household tasks (folding laundry, chopping while seated)
combined with audio learning are doubly valuable** — not just a
nice-to-have pairing, but genuinely higher-value here, since these
tasks already demand more real effort for someone with limited
mobility, making the "free" cognitive slot worth more.

### For single parents specifically

1. 🔵 **When helping a child study something the parent also wants to
relearn** (basic math, a language), the "homework help" task and the
parent's own "learn a skill" task can genuinely be the same activity
— real double-purpose overlap, from a completely different angle
than the founder's childcare-during-play insight, but the same
underlying principle (genuine overlap, not a workaround).

### For people going through grief or a major life transition

1. 🔵 **Voice-memo journaling immediately after an existing routine
moment** (e.g., during an evening walk) lowers the real barrier to
consistency for people who find sitting-down journaling too heavy in
a difficult period. This isn't a combination suggestion — it's a
*technique-level* innovation for making an Exclusive, protected task
(journaling) more sustainable to actually do, without violating the
protected-time principle (Rule 4).

### For night-shift workers

1. 🔵 **The dawn commute home needs calming content, not stimulating
content** — the opposite of standard commute-pairing logic. Suggesting
a coding podcast or an upbeat language lesson on this specific
commute would actively work against the person's sleep-hygiene goal.
Worth DoneHo explicitly recognizing shift-direction, not just
commute-existence.

### For retirees

1. 🔵 **Pair volunteering with skill-sharing/mentoring** using a
professional skill no longer used day-to-day (e.g., accounting,
teaching) — turns a leisure/social goal into something that also
rebuilds a sense of purpose. A values-based combination, distinct
from the efficiency-based logic most of this document uses.

### For newborn-stage parents (0-6 months — distinct from general new-parent life)

1. 🔵 **Shrink Exclusive-task duration to match real available windows,
not the other way round.** Sleep-deprived newborn-stage life produces
unpredictable 5-10 minute windows, not clean 30-60 minute blocks.
Rather than looking for a combination, the real fix is re-estimating
task duration itself downward — a "micro-Exclusive" mode — so a
Blueprint milestone doesn't sit permanently undone because it was
scoped for a block of time that doesn't exist in this life stage.

### For people recovering from surgery or short-term illness

1. 🔵 **Temporarily override a task's normal tier, not permanently
reclassify it.** Chopping vegetables is normally Ambient — but for
someone recovering from abdominal surgery, standing and chopping may
need to move to Exclusive-or-avoid for a defined recovery window.
This is a genuinely different mechanism from everything else in this
document: a time-bound *override* on top of the default tier, not a
new permanent classification.

### For domestic workers / household help who are themselves DoneHo users

1. 🔵 **Favor aggressively short micro-tasks (2-5 min), not
standard-length ones.** This persona's own goals (their own family's
education, their own finances) get squeezed into the smallest
fragments of a day spent working in others' homes — the gap between
employers, a wait for transport. DoneHo should default to its
shortest possible task slice for this persona rather than assume
a fuller block exists anywhere in the day.

### For joint / multi-generational households

1. 🔵 **Recognize when a task is already handled by someone else in the
household, and don't suggest it again.** If a mother-in-law already
cooks lunch daily, DoneHo shouldn't generate a separate meal-prep
milestone for that meal. Same underlying "don't double-count effort"
principle as the gig-worker fitness pattern (#2), applied to shared
domestic labor instead of personal exercise.

### For couples navigating fertility treatment

1. 🔵 **Separate the clinical task from the emotional one, and give the
emotional one a low-effort home.** Medication timing itself is
Exclusive and non-negotiable. But the emotional processing around the
journey — often suppressed because it doesn't fit as a "task" — can
be gently paired with an already-existing Ambient activity (a walk),
since this persona typically has no bandwidth for one more standalone
commitment.

### For parents of a child with special needs

1. 🔵 **Batch therapy-related admin (insurance calls, scheduling,
provider coordination) into one weekly session, not handled
reactively.** For this specific population, reactive handling creates
outsized stress compared to a general family's appointment tracking —
the hidden cost isn't the appointments themselves, it's the
fragmented admin around them.

### For long-distance relationship partners

1. 🔵 **"Parallel presence" as a lower-bandwidth substitute for full
Exclusive connection time.** Both partners doing separate but
simultaneous activities on a call (e.g., both cooking dinner while
talking) maintains real connection on days that can't support a full
dedicated video call — a genuinely different combination axis:
presence-sharing, not task-sharing.

### For people job-hunting after a layoff

1. 🔵 **Give rejection a deliberate "close the loop" moment, not silence.**
A brief acknowledgment/reset ritual immediately after checking an
application's outcome prevents that emotional residue from silently
bleeding into unrelated tasks later in the day — productivity systems
typically treat this load as invisible; naming it explicitly is the
actual innovation here.

### For startup founders / entrepreneurs

1. 🔵 **Give Exclusive-tier ideas a fast-capture path during Ambient
personal time, instead of assuming clean task boundaries.** Founders
routinely have real business thinking surface during a shower or a
walk — a one-tap voice-memo capture prevents that idea from being
lost, rather than DoneHo assuming personal and work tasks stay neatly
separated for this persona the way they might for others.

### For rotating-shift workers (beyond a fixed night shift)

1. 🔵 **"Day" needs to be user-defined, not assumed to start at
midnight.** Since this persona's shift pattern changes week to week,
a calendar-midnight day boundary makes Day Output submission timing
meaningless for them. *This has a real technical implication, not
just a suggestion pattern: DoneHo's actual day-boundary logic would
need a user-settable "day start" reference for this group to work
correctly at all.*

### For people managing seasonal mood changes or ongoing mental health self-care

1. 🔵 **Adapt the completion-rate bar itself during known difficult
periods, don't hold a flat standard year-round.** If a user has
indicated a seasonal pattern, the system's expectation of "success"
should flex accordingly — this connects directly to PRF/pacing logic
(an adaptive standard), not a combination suggestion at all.

### For solo travelers / digital nomads

1. 🔵 **Replace the weekly rhythm with an arrival-day/departure-day
rhythm.** Without a fixed home base, standard household-task patterns
(grocery, routine cleaning) don't apply the same way — errands and
admin naturally cluster around arriving in and leaving a location
instead, a genuinely different underlying rhythm than a fixed-address
week.

### For users with hearing impairment

1. 🔵 **Audio-first suggestions need a stated visual/text fallback, not
an assumption that audio serves everyone.** Since most Ambient-pairing
suggestions in this entire document default to audio content, this
needs an explicit accessibility override: captions, transcripts, or
visual timers as the substitute wherever audio is the default —
worth naming directly rather than leaving as an unstated gap.

### For people with ADHD or other attention differences

1. 🔵 **"Body doubling" — a pairing type not covered anywhere else in
this document.** Doing an Exclusive task while another person (in
person or on a quiet video call) works alongside, without direct
interaction, genuinely helps task initiation for some neurodivergent
users — even though the task's content stays fully Exclusive. This is
presence-pairing without content-sharing, a fundamentally different
mechanism from every other entry here, which pairs two active tasks.

### For caregivers of a spouse with chronic illness

1. 🔵 **Explicitly protect the caregiver's own respite time as
Exclusive, not optional.** Distinct emotional weight from elder-
parent caregiving — spousal caregivers are especially prone to
indefinitely deprioritizing their own needs. This is a guardrail-
style insight (actively protect this), not a combination insight.

### On task tiering itself — a nuance worth naming

1. 🔵 **The same task type can split into two different tiers depending
on its purpose, not its category.** E.g., a wedding-planning vendor
call: if it's informational Q&A, it's Partial and can pair with a
commute; if it's the actual decision (choosing a venue), it needs
Exclusive, undistracted attention. The tier depends on what's
actually happening in that instance, not a fixed label on "vendor
calls" as a category — worth stating as its own principle, since it
challenges the assumption that task-type alone determines tier.

## PART C — Day Boosters: technique patterns (expanded beyond founder examples)

1. 🟢 Interview-structure podcasts during Ambient tasks — founder's own.
2. 🟢 Rough weekly plans with real links/apps noted alongside — founder's own.
3. 🟢 Audio revision in the user's own voice — founder's own.
4. 🔵 Light stretch breaks between Exclusive-tier blocks — founder's own,
generalized.
5. 🟢 Large-material-to-audio conversion (the 50-PDF pattern) — founder's own.
6. 🔵 **Habit-stacking prompts** — for any new low-adherence habit, suggest
anchoring it to an existing 100%-reliable routine (see Part B, #14),
rather than a standalone reminder.
7. 🔵 **Immediate-capture prompts** — for any task prone to the "deal with
it later" failure mode (expense tracking, note-taking, todo capture),
suggest capturing at the moment of occurrence, not batching (see Part
B, #15).
8. 🔵 **Shift-aware content suggestions** — for commute/dead-time
suggestions, check whether the user is starting or ending a work
period, and suggest calming vs. energizing content accordingly (see
Part B, #19) — not a one-size-fits-all "listen to something" default.

## PART D — Smart Spend: purchase/trade-off patterns (expanded)

1. 🟢 Shopping-site comparison, cost-vs-quality by category — founder's own.
2. 🟢 Human help vs. robotic tools in cluttered homes — founder's own.
3. 🔵 One-time-use items on lower-cost platforms — founder's own, generalized.
4. 🟢 AI tool subscription bundling — founder's own.
5. 🟢 Career-related spend with multi-factor matching — founder's own.
6. 🔵 Time-saving caregiving tools always paired with a real time-vs-cost
comparison — founder's own, generalized.
7. 🔵 Kitchen efficiency tools tied to real household-size fit — founder's
own, generalized.
8. 🔵 Household organization products tied to Rule 7 — founder's own,
generalized.
9. 🔵 **Freelancer/gig-worker expense tools** — recommend receipt-capture
apps specifically built for immediate-moment logging (see Part B, #15)
over general finance apps that assume a fixed monthly review habit,
since that assumption doesn't hold for irregular-income users.
10. 🔵 **Mobility-adapted household tools** — for users with mobility
limitations, prioritize seated-use-compatible versions of household
tools (e.g., a lightweight chopper usable one-handed) over the
generic "best-rated" option, since fit-for-context matters more than
general popularity here.
